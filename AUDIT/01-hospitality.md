## Key Observations

**The revenue engine is technically sound for a project finance context but conflates unit types in ways that matter at the margin.** The platform correctly separates villa, standard suite, and double suite ADR assumptions — a meaningful improvement over single-unit models. However, a single shared `baseNights` figure for both suites, the absence of within-year seasonality distribution, and flat ADR across the projection horizon all represent simplifications that a sophisticated lender or operator will probe. The platform is not wrong — it is conservative — but it cannot currently answer the question "what happens to DSCR if July demand drops 20% but May and September hold?" because the model has no seasonal granularity.

**The bank view's DSCR-first framing is correctly calibrated for Greek bank credit culture, but three industry-standard metrics are missing.** DSCR is shown, LLCR and PLCR are computed, and the conservatism evidence (track record vs model vs market) is strong and well-presented. What a Greek bank credit committee will ask for and not immediately find: (a) RevPAR by unit type vs market — the hospitality underwriting anchor; (b) an explicit EBITDA margin % benchmarked against industry; (c) an explicit LTV covenant pass/fail alongside the DSCR covenant badge. These are table-stakes items in a hotel development credit memo and their absence will trigger follow-up requests. Each is a low-effort addition because the data is already computed.

**The FF&E reserve and pre-opening budget are modelled but buried.** Both are correctly included in the engine — FF&E reserve as a ramp schedule, pre-opening amortisation as a portfolio OPEX line. However, neither appears as an explicit named line in the bank P&L view. For a bank credit analyst, seeing FF&E reserve as a line item is important: it signals that the sponsor has a plan for asset maintenance and is not overstating free cash flow. The same applies to the pre-opening budget — its visibility as a standalone item demonstrates construction-phase cost discipline. The fix in both cases is display-only (the data is there).

**The conservatism evidence package is commercially well-constructed and a genuine differentiator.** The combination of live track record (2022–2026 actuals with YoY), model cushion cards (model below live), and the 2025 Greek Islands hotel market study comparison (suite ADR below market) is exactly what a credit committee needs to accept revenue assumptions without lengthy negotiation. The market study sourcing methodology (50/50 HIGH/MED blend, net of OTA, 13-hotel curated set) is documented in code and defensible. This is the platform's strongest domain asset and should be the entry point for any banker engagement. The only weakness is that the comparison is static (anchored to 2025 data) — implementing a live-data refresh path from Firestore would allow this section to stay current through the active season.

**The admin/bank separation is philosophically correct but has two trust-boundary gaps.** The operational viewMode split (bank view subords OpCo fees to debt service; internal view treats them as senior) is precisely what a lender expects — the sponsor is not "gaming" DSCR by hiding management fees. This is a credit credibility feature. The two gaps are: (1) the pitch deck at `/pitch` has no gate and exposes sponsor IRR/MOIC/exit multiples to any anonymous visitor, which is a disclosure risk; (2) the sponsor promote structure (EytanReturnBreakdown) is visible in the admin View-As-Banker preview mode, which should be suppressed — a bank officer does not need to see the developer's carry mechanics.

## Missing Domain Features

The following domain concepts are absent from the platform and would be expected by a Greek bank credit team reviewing a hotel development loan:

**1. STR vs GNTO licensing classification.** Greek law distinguishes between short-term rental properties (registered under the AADE digital platform registry, subject to municipal restrictions) and GNTO-classified hotels (subject to EOT licensing, which carries different legal status, minimum infrastructure requirements, and regulatory risk). If Villa Lev Group will operate as a GNTO-licensed hotel (the strong presumption for a development seeking bank financing of this scale), this classification needs to be stated explicitly in the bank view. It removes STR regulatory risk from the credit analysis entirely.

**2. Operator selection and management contract terms.** The model has OpCo fee economics but no operator identity or contract type. Banks financing hotel development in Greece routinely require a management agreement or at minimum a heads-of-terms from a proposed operator. For an independent operation (no franchise), the bank will want to understand the operator's track record and the contract's term and termination provisions. For a branded operation, the bank assesses the brand's contribution to ADR and occupancy against the franchise fee cost. Neither is currently represented.

**3. Pre-opening budget as a named CAPEX/OPEX category in the bank view.** The pre-opening cost is modelled (`portfolioOpex.preOpeningTotal`) and amortised, but it is not presented as a standalone budget item in the bank-facing output. A typical pre-opening budget for a luxury boutique of this scale (15+ keys) would include: marketing and PR pre-launch (€60–100K), staff recruitment and training (€80–120K), operating supplies initial stock (€40–60K), soft-opening operating losses, and system setup. The bank will want to confirm these costs are fully funded and not squeezing the equity contribution.

**4. Construction milestone schedule linked to drawdowns.** The model captures the total construction CAPEX and the VAT cashflow timing, but it does not present a milestone-linked drawdown schedule showing what physical condition the building must be in before each tranche is released. This is standard in Greek bank construction finance — the technical review (technical advisor / quantity surveyor sign-off at each milestone) is a condition precedent to each draw. Presenting the milestone schedule with planned dates and % of CAPEX associated with each stage would materially strengthen the credit memo.

**5. ADR growth assumption.** The current model assumes flat nominal ADR for the entire 12-year horizon. In the context of Paros/Antiparos — a high-demand, supply-constrained luxury destination with documented annual ADR growth — this is conservative to the point of understating the long-run investment case. The admin view should offer an optional ADR growth rate assumption (even if the bank view is locked at 0%) to allow the operator to see the return differential. It also allows the bank to test upside scenarios in their own credit committee.

**6. F&B revenue model.** Ancillary revenue is modelled as a net profit lump sum. For a multi-villa + hotel-room development of this scale, a bank credit analyst will ask specifically about F&B: is there a restaurant? A beach club? Poolside service? Is F&B revenue included in the ancillary line? What are the associated costs? The absence of a dedicated F&B treatment — even if it simply documents that F&B profit is captured in the ancillary net figure — will generate a due diligence question.
